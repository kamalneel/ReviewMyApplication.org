## 2. Academic Records (Mandatory)

> **Required by: ALL schools — The most important factor in admissions**

### 2.1 High School Transcript
| Element | Required | Notes |
|---------|----------|-------|
| Official Transcript | ✅ Mandatory | Sent by school, not student |
| All Courses & Grades (9-12) | ✅ Mandatory | Full academic record |
| Mid-Year Report | ✅ Mandatory | Current senior year grades |
| Final Transcript | Post-Admission | Sent after graduation |

### 2.2 Course Requirements (Recommended Minimums)
> Most selective colleges expect **more** than these minimums:

| Subject | Years Required | Years Recommended | Top College Expectation |
|---------|----------------|-------------------|------------------------|
| English | 4 years | 4 years | 4 years (AP/Honors when possible) |
| Mathematics | 3 years | 4 years | Through Calculus (AB or BC) |
| Science | 2-3 years | 4 years | 3-4 with labs (including AP) |
| Social Studies/History | 2-3 years | 4 years | Including AP courses |
| Foreign Language | 2 years | 3-4 years | 4 years same language preferred |
| Visual/Performing Arts | 1 year | 1-2 years | Shows well-roundedness |
| Computer Science | 0 | 1-2 courses | Increasingly valued |

### 2.3 California A-G Requirements (for UC/CSU)
> **Required for UC and CSU admission:**

| Category | Subject | Years Required |
|----------|---------|----------------|
| A | History/Social Science | 2 years |
| B | English | 4 years |
| C | Mathematics | 3 years (4 recommended) |
| D | Laboratory Science | 2 years (3 recommended) |
| E | Language Other Than English | 2 years (3 recommended) |
| F | Visual and Performing Arts | 1 year |
| G | College-Preparatory Elective | 1 year |

**GPA Requirements:**
- UC: Minimum 3.0 GPA (California residents), 3.4 (non-residents)
- CSU: Minimum 2.5 GPA (California residents), 3.0 (non-residents)

### 2.4 Course Rigor Evaluation
> Colleges evaluate the **rigor** of your curriculum:

| Course Level | Weight | Notes |
|--------------|--------|-------|
| Regular/Standard | Baseline | |
| Honors | Higher | Shows willingness to challenge |
| AP (Advanced Placement) | Highest | College-level work |
| IB (International Baccalaureate) | Highest | Rigorous international curriculum |
| Dual Enrollment/College Courses | Highest | Actual college credit |

### 2.5 GPA Considerations
| Metric | Importance | Notes |
|--------|------------|-------|
| Unweighted GPA | High | On 4.0 scale |
| Weighted GPA | High | Accounts for course rigor |
| Core GPA | High | Academic subjects only |
| GPA Trend | Medium | Improvement valued |
| Class Rank | Medium | If school provides |

### 2.6 Typical Academic Profile at Top Schools
| School Tier | Typical Unweighted GPA | Typical Weighted GPA |
|-------------|------------------------|----------------------|
| Ivy League / Stanford / MIT | 3.9-4.0 | 4.0+ |
| Top 20 Universities | 3.8-4.0 | 4.0+ |

| UC Berkeley / UCLA | 3.9-4.0 (unweighted) | 4.0+ |

## 3. Standardized Testing (Evolving Landscape)

> **Major changes post-2024: Many schools reinstating requirements**

### 3.1 Current Testing Landscape (2025-2026)

| Policy | Description | Schools |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Test Required** | Must submit SAT or ACT | Harvard, Yale, MIT, Dartmouth, Brown, Georgetown, Purdue, Georgia Tech |
| **Test Optional** | Can choose whether to submit | Many schools; check each policy |
| **Test Free** | Scores not considered even if submitted | UC System, Caltech, Pitzer |
| **Test Flexible** | Accept alternatives (AP, IB, etc.) | Some schools |

### 3.2 SAT (College Board)
| Section | Score Range | Time | Content |
|---------|-------------|------|---------|
| Reading and Writing | 200-800 | 64 min | Reading comprehension, grammar, vocabulary |
| Math | 200-800 | 70 min | Algebra, geometry, data analysis, advanced math |
| **Total Score** | 400-1600 | 2 hr 14 min | Combined score |

**Score Submission:**
- Score Choice: Send only scores you want (most schools)
- Superscoring: Many schools combine best section scores across dates

### 3.3 ACT
| Section | Score Range | Time | Content |
|---------|-------------|------|---------|
| English | 1-36 | 45 min | Grammar, punctuation, rhetorical skills |
| Math | 1-36 | 60 min | Pre-algebra through trigonometry |
| Reading | 1-36 | 35 min | Reading comprehension |
| Science | 1-36 | 35 min | Scientific reasoning |
| Writing (Optional) | 2-12 | 40 min | Essay (few schools require) |
| **Composite Score** | 1-36 | 2 hr 55 min | Average of four sections |

### 3.4 Typical Test Scores at Top Schools
| School Tier | SAT Middle 50% | ACT Middle 50% |
|-------------|----------------|----------------|
| Ivy League / Stanford / MIT | 1500-1570 | 34-36 |
| Top 20 Universities | 1450-1550 | 33-35 |
| Top 30-50 Universities | 1400-1520 | 32-34 |
| UC Berkeley | 1400-1530 | 31-35 |
| UCLA | 1390-1540 | 31-35 |

### 3.5 AP Exams (Advanced Placement)
| Score | Meaning | College Credit |
|-------|---------|----------------|
| 5 | Extremely well qualified | Usually credit/placement |
| 4 | Well qualified | Often credit/placement |
| 3 | Qualified | Sometimes credit |
| 2 | Possibly qualified | Rarely credit |
| 1 | No recommendation | No credit |

> **Top colleges expect multiple AP courses with scores of 4 or 5**

### 3.6 Subject Tests & Other Exams
| Test | Status | Notes |
|------|--------|-------|
| SAT Subject Tests | Discontinued (2021) | No longer offered |
| AP Exams | Ongoing | Recommended for rigor |
| IB Exams | Ongoing | For IB Diploma students |
| TOEFL/IELTS | International students | English proficiency |
| Duolingo English Test | Alternative | Accepted by many schools |

## 4. Extracurricular Activities (Critical)

> **One of the most important factors for selective admissions**

### 4.1 Activity List Requirements
Most applications allow 8-10 activities with:

| Field | Required | Character Limit |
|-------|----------|-----------------|
| Activity Type | ✅ Mandatory | Dropdown selection |
| Position/Leadership | ✅ Mandatory | 50 characters |
| Organization Name | ✅ Mandatory | 100 characters |
| Activity Description | ✅ Mandatory | 150 characters |
| Grade Levels | ✅ Mandatory | 9, 10, 11, 12 checkboxes |
| Timing | ✅ Mandatory | School year, Break, All year |
| Hours/Week | ✅ Mandatory | Number |
| Weeks/Year | ✅ Mandatory | Number |
| Continue in College | Optional | Yes/No |

### 4.2 Activity Quality Tiers
> Colleges evaluate **depth over breadth**:

| Tier | Description | Examples |
|------|-------------|----------|
| **Tier 1: Exceptional/National** | Rare achievements, national/international recognition | National competition winner, published author, patent holder, recruited athlete, professional-level achievement |
| **Tier 2: State/Regional Impact** | Significant achievement with broader recognition | State competition finalist, founded organization with impact, significant leadership role |
| **Tier 3: Significant School/Local** | Notable involvement with leadership | Club president, varsity captain, lead in school play, Eagle Scout |
| **Tier 4: Participation** | Consistent involvement without leadership | Club member, sports participant, regular volunteer |

### 4.3 Activity Categories
| Category | Examples |
|----------|----------|
| **Academic** | Research, academic competitions (Science Olympiad, MATHCOUNTS, Debate, Model UN), tutoring |
| **Arts** | Visual arts, music (band, orchestra, solo), theater, dance, film, creative writing |
| **Athletics** | Varsity/JV/Club sports, individual sports, martial arts |
| **Community Service** | Volunteering, service organizations, community projects |
| **Work/Employment** | Jobs, internships, family responsibilities |
| **Family Responsibilities** | Caregiving, family business, household duties |
| **Leadership** | Student government, club officers, team captain |
| **Cultural/Religious** | Cultural organizations, religious groups, heritage activities |
| **Entrepreneurship** | Started business, app development, social enterprise |
| **Research/STEM** | Scientific research, engineering projects, coding projects |

### 4.4 What Top Colleges Look For
| Quality | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Depth over Breadth** | 2-3 significant commitments > 10 superficial activities |
| **Sustained Commitment** | Multi-year involvement, not just junior/senior year |
| **Leadership & Initiative** | Roles that show impact and responsibility |
| **Impact** | What changed because of your involvement? |
| **Passion** | Authentic interest, not just resume-building |
| **Coherent Narrative** | Activities that tell a story about who you are |

### 4.5 Honors & Awards Section
Most applications have separate section for honors (typically 5 slots):

| Field | Notes |
|-------|-------|
| Honor Title | Specific name of award |
| Grade Level(s) | When received |
| Level of Recognition | School, State/Regional, National, International |
| Description (if needed) | Brief context |

**Examples of Strong Honors:**
- National Merit Finalist/Scholar
- AP Scholar with Distinction
- State/National competition placement
- Published work
- Significant scholarship awards
- Leadership awards (Boys/Girls State, etc.)

## 5. Application Essays (Critical)

> **Essays are where you distinguish yourself from equally qualified applicants**

### 5.1 Common Application Essay (Required)
**2024-2025 Prompts (250-650 words):**

1. "Some students have a background, identity, interest, or talent that is so meaningful they believe their application would be incomplete without it. If this sounds like you, then please share your story."

2. "The lessons we take from obstacles we encounter can be fundamental to later success. Recount a time when you faced a challenge, setback, or failure. How did it affect you, and what did you learn from the experience?"

3. "Reflect on a time when you questioned or challenged a belief or idea. What prompted your thinking? What was the outcome?"

4. "Reflect on something that someone has done for you that has made you happy or thankful in a surprising way. How has this gratitude affected or motivated you?"

5. "Discuss an accomplishment, event, or realization that sparked a period of personal growth and a new understanding of yourself or others."

6. "Describe a topic, idea, or concept you find so engaging that it makes you lose all track of time. Why does it captivate you? What or who do you turn to when you want to learn more?"

7. "Share an essay on any topic of your choice. It can be one you've already written, one that responds to a different prompt, or one of your own design."

### 5.2 Supplemental Essays by School Type

#### Why This School/Why This Major
| Example Prompts | Word Limit | Notes |
|-----------------|------------|-------|
| "Why are you interested in [University]?" | 150-400 | Must be specific |
| "Why do you want to study [major] at [University]?" | 100-300 | Connect interests to programs |
| "How will you contribute to our community?" | 100-250 | Show fit |

#### Intellectual Curiosity
| Example Prompts | Word Limit | Notes |
|-----------------|------------|-------|
| "Tell us about something that has sparked your intellectual curiosity" | 250-400 | Show genuine passion |
| "Describe a book/idea that changed how you think" | 100-300 | Demonstrate reading/thinking |

#### Community/Diversity
| Example Prompts | Word Limit | Notes |
|-----------------|------------|-------|
| "How will you contribute to diversity on campus?" | 150-350 | Beyond just demographics |
| "Describe a community you belong to" | 100-300 | What you give and receive |

#### Quirky/Creative
| Example Prompts | Word Limit | Notes |
|-----------------|------------|-------|
| "What would you teach a class on?" | 100-200 | Shows personality |
| "If you could have dinner with anyone..." | 100-200 | Reveals interests |

### 5.3 School-Specific Essay Examples

#### Stanford University
- Short Essays (50 words each): What is most meaningful to you and why? The most significant challenge... What historical moment would you witness?
- Short Essays (100-250 words): Describes about 5 topics including intellectual vitality, meaningful activities, interests
- Roommate Letter (100-250 words): Write a note to your future roommate

#### MIT
- Essays on activities, department/course interest, how you contribute to community, something you do for fun, world you come from

#### Princeton
- Graded written paper from junior or senior year
- Short answer questions
- "Your Voice" essay: 250-word response to a quote
- Essay on extracurricular or work experience (250 words)
- Essay on topic you'd teach (250 words)

#### UC System (Personal Insight Questions)
- Choose 4 of 8 prompts
- 350 words each
- Topics: Leadership, creativity, talent, education opportunity, significant challenge, favorite subject, community impact, what makes you stand out

### 5.4 Essay Best Practices
| Do | Don't |
|----|-------|
| Be authentic and personal | Write what you think they want to hear |
| Show, don't tell | List achievements already in application |
| Be specific and concrete | Be vague or generic |
| Have a clear point/message | Try to cover too much |
| Reveal something new about you | Repeat information from other parts |
| Proofread carefully | Submit with errors |
| Start early and revise | Write the night before |
| Get feedback (but keep your voice) | Have others rewrite for you |

## 6. Letters of Recommendation (Mandatory)

> **Required by: ALL selective schools**

### 6.1 Required Recommendations
| Type | Number | Who | Notes |
|------|--------|-----|-------|
| Counselor Recommendation | 1 | School counselor/college advisor | Provides context, academic summary |
| Teacher Recommendations | 2 | Core academic teachers | Usually 11th/12th grade teachers |
| Additional Recommendations | 0-2 | Optional | Coach, mentor, employer, etc. |

### 6.2 Teacher Recommendation Guidelines
| Guideline | Details |
|-----------|---------|
| **Who to Ask** | Teachers who know you well, from core academic subjects |
| **When to Ask** | End of junior year or very early senior year |
| **Best Subjects** | English, Math, Science, History, Foreign Language |
| **Ideal Teacher** | One who has seen your growth, can speak to character |
| **What to Provide** | Resume, brag sheet, specific anecdotes, why you're asking them |

### 6.3 What Recommenders Address
| Area | Questions Considered |
|------|---------------------|
| Academic Ability | Intellectual curiosity, work ethic, class participation |
| Character | Integrity, maturity, leadership |
| Contribution | Impact on class, collaboration, helping others |
| Context | Growth, overcoming challenges, unique circumstances |
| Potential | What student will contribute to college community |

### 6.4 School-Specific Recommendation Requirements
| School | Requirements |
|--------|-------------|
| Most Schools | Counselor + 2 Teachers |
| MIT | 2 Teacher recs (1 math/science, 1 humanities) |
| Caltech | 2 Teacher recs (1 math/science required) |
| Georgetown | 1 Teacher from senior year, core subject |
| Arts Schools | May require arts teacher/instructor |

### 6.5 Additional/Supplemental Recommendations
| When to Use | Examples |
|-------------|----------|
| Significant extracurricular | Coach for recruited athletes |
| Unique context | Mentor from research, internship |
| Special talent | Music teacher, art instructor |
| Work experience | Employer |
| Community involvement | Volunteer coordinator, religious leader |

> **Warning:** More is NOT always better. Only add if it provides new, meaningful information.

## 11. Application Logistics (Mandatory)

### 11.1 Application Timeline
| Timeframe | Activity |
|-----------|----------|
| **Summer before Senior Year** | Finalize college list, start essays, prepare activity list |
| **August-September** | Visit campuses, finalize list, request recommendations |
| **October 1** | FAFSA opens; CSS Profile available |
| **October-November** | Submit Early Decision/Early Action applications |
| **November 1-15** | Early Decision/Action deadlines (most schools) |
| **November 30** | UC Application deadline |
| **December 15-20** | Early Decision/Action decisions released |
| **January 1-15** | Regular Decision deadlines |
| **February** | Submit mid-year reports |
| **March-April** | Regular Decision results released |
| **May 1** | National Decision Day (enrollment deposit due) |
| **May-June** | Send final transcript, AP scores |

### 11.2 Application Rounds
| Round | Deadline | Decision | Binding? | Strategic Use |
|-------|----------|----------|----------|---------------|
| **Early Decision (ED)** | Nov 1-15 | Mid-December | Yes | Top choice only |
| **Early Decision II (ED2)** | Jan 1-15 | Mid-February | Yes | If not accepted ED1 |
| **Early Action (EA)** | Nov 1-15 | Mid-December | No | Show interest |
| **Restrictive EA (REA/SCEA)** | Nov 1 | Mid-December | No* | Top choice (Harvard, Stanford, etc.) |
| **Regular Decision (RD)** | Jan 1-15 | March-April | No | Standard round |
| **Rolling Admission** | Varies | 4-8 weeks | No | Apply early for best results |

*Restricts other early applications to some schools

### 11.3 How Many Schools to Apply To
| Strategy | Number | Notes |
|----------|--------|-------|
| Conservative | 6-8 schools | 2 reach, 3-4 match, 2 safety |
| Moderate | 10-12 schools | 4 reach, 4-5 match, 2-3 safety |
| Aggressive | 15-20 schools | For highly competitive applicants |

## Appendix B: Essay Writing Guide

### Personal Statement Brainstorming Questions
1. What is a core value you hold? How did you develop it?
2. What experience has shaped who you are today?
3. What makes you different from other applicants?
4. What do you love doing so much you lose track of time?
5. What obstacle have you overcome that taught you something important?
6. What would your best friend say is most unique about you?
7. What topic could you talk about for hours?

### Supplemental Essay Strategies

#### "Why Us" Essays
- Research specific programs, professors, courses
- Connect YOUR interests to THEIR offerings
- Mention campus culture, traditions, values
- Show you'd contribute, not just take

#### Activity/Elaboration Essays
- Pick your most meaningful activity
- Tell a specific story, not a summary
- Show impact and growth
- Connect to future goals

### Common Essay Mistakes
| Mistake | Why It Hurts |
|---------|--------------|
| Writing what you think they want to hear | Comes across as inauthentic |
| Summarizing achievements | Already in application |
| Using clichés | Shows lack of original thinking |
| Being too vague | Forgettable, interchangeable |
| Trying to sound impressive | Often backfires |
| Not answering the prompt | Shows carelessness |
| Submitting with errors | Suggests you don't care |

## Appendix D: Timeline for High-Achieving Students

### Freshman Year (9th Grade)
- Take challenging courses appropriate for your level
- Explore extracurricular activities
- Develop strong study habits
- Start thinking about interests

### Sophomore Year (10th Grade)
- Increase rigor (honors, pre-AP courses)
- Deepen 2-3 extracurricular commitments
- Consider PSAT practice
- Explore potential majors/careers

### Junior Year (11th Grade)
- Take AP/IB courses in areas of strength
- Take PSAT/NMSQT (October)
- Take SAT/ACT (spring)
- Begin college research
- Attend college fairs, visit campuses
- Develop relationships with recommenders
- Start essay brainstorming
- Take AP exams (May)
- Create initial college list

### Senior Year (12th Grade)
- Finalize college list (August)
- Request recommendations (September)
- Complete applications (October-January)
- Apply for financial aid (October onward)
- Complete interviews (fall-winter)
- Submit mid-year reports (February)
- Make final decision (by May 1)
- Send final transcript (June)
